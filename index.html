01

The core equation

Every choice the site makes reduces to one number: actual BUTc produced per building. Everything else is either a component of that number or a constraint on it.

BUTcactual = BUTcperfect × ( 1 − Loss )

Two levers move BUTc: how much you could produce (Perfect), and how much you actually keep after inefficiency (Loss). The rest of this page is about what determines each of those.

02

Perfect BUTc — the ceiling

Perfect BUTc is what your GPUs would produce with zero cooling loss. It's determined entirely by what you put in your racks.

6060 GPU
0.8 BUTc per GPU
9090 GPU
2.0 BUTc per GPU — 2.5× the 6060 rate
Rack capacity
10 GPUs
BUTcperfect = racks × 10 GPUs × BUTcper GPU

A rack full of 6060s tops out at 8 BUTc. A rack full of 9090s tops out at 20 BUTc. The infrastructure cost is identical — same power, same heat, same cooling. That means if you can afford 9090s, always use them: 2.5× the output for the exact same rack + AC footprint.

03

The loss curve — where diminishing returns come from

Loss is what makes this game interesting. It's not linear — each additional AC cools a smaller share of the remaining inefficiency. The site's loss formula was fit from real in-game data:

// Original data: single 6060 rack (perfect = 8 BUTc) 2 Small ACs → 7.79 BUTc (2.6% loss) 4 Small ACs → 7.89 BUTc (1.4% loss) 8 Small ACs → 7.94 BUTc (0.7% loss) 22 Small ACs → 7.98 BUTc (0.25% loss)

Fitting a curve to those points and simplifying into raw cooling units (each AC delivers a known amount of cooling), the formula falls out cleanly:

Loss = 1 / ( 2 y + 1 )    where y = cooling per rack
// Loss vs cooling per rack — the ~13% sweet spot marked

Look at the shape: from 0 to ~3 cooling per rack, loss drops fast. Beyond that, the curve flattens — you're paying more and more cooling for smaller and smaller gains. That flatness is the key insight behind the sweet spot in section 07.

04

Cooling delivery — the two ACs

Only two units cool the building. Their per-unit stats look different, but their efficiency ratios are identical:

Small AC
9 cooling / 4 power = 2.25 cooling per power
Big AC
18 cooling / 8 power = 2.25 cooling per power

A Big AC is exactly two Small ACs in one box. Choose based on floor space and in-game price, never on efficiency — the math treats them the same.

Coolingtotal = 9 × Smalls + 18 × Bigs = 2.25 × AC power

All cooling from all ACs is pooled across the whole building. That's why the Loss Snapshot page takes total cooling as one number: your specific AC mix doesn't change anything, only the total does.

y = Coolingtotal / racks    (cooling per rack, feeds the loss formula)
05

Power & heat — the constraints

Every unit in the building costs power. Every rack produces heat. Two hard limits.

Rack
10 power drawn, 2 heat produced (independent of GPU count)
Small AC
4 power drawn
Big AC
8 power drawn
// Power budget (must fit under building cap) 10 R + 4 S + 8 B ≤ Pmax // Heat balance (cooling must cover heat) 9 S + 18 B + C0 ≥ 2 R + H0

C0 and H0 are baseline cooling and heat already present in the building from other equipment. In most cases both are zero, and at the optimum cooling naturally exceeds heat — so heat almost never binds. Power is the constraint that actually shapes the answer.

06

The optimization problem

Put it all together. We want to maximize actual BUTc, subject to the power and heat constraints, choosing how many racks (R) and how much AC power (Pac) to build.

// Maximize BUTc = R × 10 × b × ( 1 − 1 / ( 2 y + 1 )) // Where y = ( 2.25 × Pac + C0 ) / R b = BUTc per GPU // Subject to 10 R + Pac ≤ Pavail (power) 2.25 Pac + C0 ≥ 2 R + H0 (heat)

Since BUTc is monotonically increasing in y (more cooling → less loss, always), the optimizer uses all available power: Pac = Pavail − 10R. That reduces the problem to a single variable: how many racks?

07

Solving for the sweet spot

Substitute the power constraint into the cooling equation. Define K = 2.25 Pavail + C0 (the total cooling budget, converted to power-equivalent units). Then:

y = K / R − 22.5

Plug into the BUTc equation and simplify. After the algebra:

BUTc(R) = 10 b × R × ( 2 K − 45 R ) / ( 2 K − 44 R )

Take the derivative with respect to R, set it to zero, and after multiplying through you land on a clean quadratic:

495 R2 − 45 K R + K2 = 0

The quadratic formula gives two roots. The physically meaningful one (the other gives negative cooling):

R* = K × ( 45 − 3√5 ) / 990 ≈ 0.03868 × K

At R*, cooling per rack works out to:

y* = √11.25 ≈ 3.354
Loss* = 1 / ( 2√11.25 + 1 ) ≈ 12.98%

Notice the constants: the sweet spot depends only on the game's fixed ratios (rack power, AC efficiency, loss curve shape). It's not a function of how big your building is. Every building — from 100 racks to 10,000 — optimizes at the same ~3.354 cooling per rack.

08

Why 13% loss beats 1% (when power is fixed)

The counter-intuitive part: lower loss isn't always better. It looks like 1% loss should give more BUTc than 13% loss — 99% vs 87% output per rack. But that ignores where the cooling comes from: power that could've bought more racks.

Concrete comparison at a 5,000 power cap:

// Strategy A: chase 1.5% loss 96 racks × 10 pwr = 960 rack pwr Remaining 4,040 pwr → 1,010 Smalls → 9,090 cooling y = 94.7, Loss = 0.5% BUTc = 96 × 20 × 0.995 = 1,910 (9090s) // Strategy B: embrace 13% loss 435 racks × 10 pwr = 4,350 rack pwr Remaining 650 pwr → 162 Smalls → 1,458 cooling y = 3.35, Loss = 13.0% BUTc = 435 × 20 × 0.87 = 7,569 (9090s)

Strategy B produces 4× as much BUTc despite the higher loss %. The extra 339 racks earning at 87% efficiency crush the 96 racks earning at 99.5%. Loss % is a rate, not a quantity — and rates only matter when you multiply them by the base they're operating on.

The rule: chase lower loss only when you can't add more racks (racks are the bottleneck). When power is the bottleneck — which is nearly always in this game — settle at ~13% loss and use freed power for racks.

09

Building expansion — when to add a second

A common intuition trap: "my building has too many racks, the falloff must be getting bad — I should start fresh in a new building." This is wrong. The loss formula uses a ratio (cooling per rack), not a size. A building with 400 racks and 1,340 cooling has the exact same loss % as one with 40 racks and 134 cooling.

// Two buildings at power P each = one building at 2P for BUTc output 2 × ( BUTc at P ) = BUTc at 2P // no penalty, no bonus

So the decision to expand is entirely about the power cap. Rules the site enforces:

  • Under 70% of cap: add racks + ACs inside your current building. Same efficiency as a new building, no construction cost.
  • Near cap but suboptimal: rebuild first. Rebalancing racks/ACs at the same power draw is free BUTc.
  • At cap and running efficient: now expand. A second building is the only way past the power ceiling.

The Loss Snapshot's Panel 04 runs this check live and tells you which state you're in.

10

Why the two AC solvers differ by 1

If you carry results between the AC Calculator (Solver 02) and Optimal Setup (Solver 03), you'll notice the AC counts differ by 1 for the same underlying scenario. That's intentional — they round in opposite directions because they're solving opposite questions:

AC Calculator
Rounds AC counts UP. Guarantees you hit your target loss % — may slightly exceed the power cap.
Optimal Setup
Rounds AC counts DOWN. Guarantees you stay within the power budget — may miss the theoretical loss target by a tiny amount.

Because cooling can't be fractional (you either buy an AC or you don't), one of these rounding directions always applies. Pick the solver whose guarantee matches what you care about: hitting the loss target vs. staying within power. All the derived numbers (loss %, cooling per rack, actual BUTc) match between the two once the AC counts are the same.
